Reading thermal comfort
off a human face.

A from-scratch reproduction of Bai et al.'s non-intrusive thermal-comfort model. No wearables, no contact sensors — just the skin temperature of six facial regions, read from infrared images, mapped to whether a person wants it cooler, neutral, or warmer.

Implements: Bai, Liu, Liu, Yu, Shen & Sun (2024), “Non-intrusive personal thermal comfort modeling: A machine learning approach using infrared face recognition.” doi:10.1016/j.buildenv.2023.111033

The idea

Comfort you can measure without touching anyone

HVAC systems run on one set-point for a whole room, yet people in that room disagree about whether they're too hot or too cold. This project rebuilds a data-driven alternative: an infrared camera reads facial skin temperature from a distance, and a classifier infers each person's thermal preference. Everything here runs on the public Charlotte-ThermalFace dataset, so the whole pipeline is reproducible end to end.

Module 1 — skin temperature extraction
DATA
Thermal images
10 subjects, 10 camera distances, 16-bit radiometric TIFFs.
LANDMARKS
73 key points
Facial landmarks → polygon per region (43 points for side faces).
ROI
6 regions → °C
Mean temperature per region via TLinear (raw/100 − 273.15 K).
Module 2 — thermal comfort modeling
SELECT
RF + GBDT importance
Model uses cheeks, eyes, nose, mouth (forehead & chin dropped, per paper).
TRAIN
11 classifiers
6 traditional + 4 ensemble + Broad Learning, 80/20 split.
EVALUATE
By model & by distance
Precision, recall, F1, macro-F1 with 5-fold CV.

How this lines up with the original paper

Confirmed. The nose runs coldest (32.87 °C here), ensemble models clearly beat the weakest linear baselines (Logistic Regression, Naive Bayes), the neutral class is the hardest to call, and accuracy decays steadily as the subject sits farther from the camera.

Diverged — feature importance. The model still uses cheeks, eyes, nose and mouth, but that set follows the paper by design; this run's own importances don't reproduce the paper's ranking. Cheek dominates (RF 0.31, GBDT 0.62), while mouth — one of the kept regions — actually scores lowest in both, and forehead and chin are not clearly the weakest. The four-region choice rests on the paper's precedent more than on this data.

Diverged — models. The paper reports Broad Learning and DCF as joint-best, with BL precision near 90%. In this reproduction every model sits in a tighter, lower band (macro-F1 ≈ 0.59–0.74), XGBoost leads, and BL is mid-pack. The likeliest causes: a stricter held-out CV protocol instead of training-set scores, a corrected TLinear scale and reconstructed ROI polygons, and the absence of DCF in this environment. BL still trains fast and stays competitive — its original selling point holds even if its top ranking doesn't.
